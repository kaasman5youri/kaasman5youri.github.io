    Intro

    The Knight’s Tale This story is written by Geoffrey Chaucer and is one story of the 24 completed stories in the Canterbury Tales. The knight is a noble man, of highest social standing, and so he tells an epic story of a chivalric romance. Rhyme Scheme: AA-BB-CC-…. (Only part I will be analysed for literary devices)

    Summary part1:

    Introduction of duke Theseus, Theseus was the lord of Athens. He was married to the queen Hippolyta. Emily was Hippolyta`s younger sister. Theseus was going home and came across a group of women, clothed in black, who complained that they lost their husbands in battle. Theseus promises that he will take revenge and defeat Creon, the lord of Thebes Theseus defeates king Creon. While pillaging Theseus and his fellows find two young knights, Arcita and Palamon. Palamon and Arcita where captured and imprisoned. Arcita wants a relationship with Emily and compares her with Venus (godess of beauty), but Palamon says that he already loves Emily. The quarrel, Arcita says that he loved Emily first and Palomon claimed he was first. Duke Pirithous, the friend of duke Theseus, knew Arcita and asked if he could be released. Arcita was released but he is not allowed to be in the country of Theseus. Arcita regrets losing sight on Emily, while Palamon pities himself still being in prison. The Knight asks which is the (un)luckiest...

    Analysis Canterbury Tales, the Knight’s Tale – Geoffrey Chaucer (1387)

    Once, as old histories tell us,

    There was a duke who was called Theseus;

    He was lord and governor of Athens,

    And in his time such a conqueror

    That there was no one greater under the sun.

    Hyperbole:

    The story is almost entirely written in hyperbole: A dramatic tale about the sufferers of love. 863 “That gretter was ther noon under the sonne.”.

    Very many a powerful country had he won;

    What with his wisdom and his chivalry

    Alliteration:

    865 “What with his wysdom”.

    He conquered all the land of the Amazons,

    That once was called Scithia,

    And wedded the queen Ypolita,

    And brought her home with him into his country

    Alliteration:

    869 “hire hoom with hym in his”.

    With much glory and great ceremony,

    And also her young sister Emelye.

    And thus with victory and with festivity

    I leave this noble duke riding to Athens,

    And all his host in arms beside him.

    And certainly, if it were not too long to hear,

    I would have told you fully the manner

    How the reign of Femenye was won

    By Theseus and by his chivalry;

    Preterition:

    =disregarding a matter (or at least pretending to). 873 “Lete I this noble duc to Atthenes ryde, 874 And al his hoost in armes hym bisyde. 875 And certes, if it nere to long to heere, 876 I wolde have toold yow fully the manere” (And he continues to tell about it in 877-884).

    And of the great battle at that time

    Enumeration:

    The knight sums up what he wasn’t going to tell about. “And of the grete battaille…” And how asseged was Ypolita, And of the feste…” And of the tempest…” (Enumeration with polysyndeton (=repetition of conjunctions, like “and”)).

    Between Athenians and Amazons;

    Alliteration:

    880 “Atthenes en Amazons”.

    And how Ypolita was besieged,

    The fair, bold queen of Scithia;

    Epithet:

    An 'decorative' description. They were regularly used by classical writers: they often used such an ornamental addition as a permanent designation with the names of heroes and gods. 882 “The faire, hardy queene of Scithia;”. = The wife of Theseus, queen of the Amazones (She will repeatedly be called like this).

    But al that thyng I moot as now forbere.

    I have, God knows, a large field to till,

    And the oxen in my plow are weak.

    The remnant of the tale is long enough.

    Metaphor

    The knight uses a plough as a metaphor for the heavy task of telling the story: 886 “I have, God woot, a large feeld to ere, 887 And wayke been the oxen in my plough. 888 The remenant of the tale is long ynough." .

    This duke, of whom I make mention,

    Each after another, clad in black clothes;

    But such a cry and such a woeful (lament) they make

    That in this world is no living creature

    That (ever) heard lamentation such as this;
